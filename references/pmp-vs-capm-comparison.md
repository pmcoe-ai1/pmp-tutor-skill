# PMP vs CAPM — Comparison

When a student is choosing between PMP and CAPM, load this file.

## Decision logic

### Take PMP if

- 36+ months of project leadership experience (with degree) or 60+ months (without).
- Currently in a project management role, or moving into one.
- Employer recognises and rewards PMP (most large enterprises in tech, finance, healthcare, federal contracting do).
- Cost of training plus exam is < 10% of expected annual salary uplift.

### Take CAPM first if

- Less than 36 months of project experience.
- Career-pivoting from non-PM into PM (CAPM signals commitment to recruiters).
- Working in a project-adjacent role (business analyst, scrum team member, ops) where PMP-level expertise isn't yet expected.
- Education-budget constrained; CAPM is the cheaper credential to test the credential's career value before committing to PMP later.

### Skip both if

- Industry doesn't value the credential (most pure-tech roles, most non-Western contexts, many small-business contexts).
- The certification is treated as the goal rather than the signal — if the student wants project management skills, the PMI exam is one path among many; the PMP is the credential.
- The student needs to ship a specific project on a deadline and certification time would delay it.

## The CAPM-then-PMP path

A common pattern for career-pivoters: CAPM at year 1, PMP at year 4 (when experience requirement is met).

- Pro: builds credential signal early; demonstrates progression to recruiters.
- Con: pays for both exams ($225 + $425 = $650 in PMI fees plus training).
- Pro: 23 hours of CAPM contact hours can be applied toward the 35-hour PMP requirement at PMI's discretion (verify with PMI before assuming this).

## What the skill should say

When the student asks "PMP or CAPM?":
1. Ask about their project experience (months) and degree.
2. Map to the decision logic above.
3. Be direct: "Based on what you've told me, you should sit {X} now."
4. If the situation is genuinely borderline, walk through both paths and let the student decide.

When the student asks "is PMP worth it for me?":
1. Ask about their current role, industry, and career goal.
2. Be honest: PMP value depends heavily on industry, employer, and career stage. Don't oversell.
3. Offer a generic decision framework, not an industry-specific promise.

## What to NEVER say

- "PMP guarantees a promotion / salary increase / job offer." — It doesn't. PMI's published salary uplift is a median, not a personal projection.
- "You should take PMP because it's harder / more prestigious." — Prestige is irrelevant if the student doesn't have the experience for it; CAPM is sometimes the right answer even when PMP looks fancier.
- "PMCOE will get you through PMP easily." — Per project policy, no pass-guarantee phrasing.
